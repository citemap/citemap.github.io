Federal Judge Voids 'Conscience Rule'

Policy would have allowed providers to refuse care because of "moral objections"

MedpageToday
  • share to facebook
  • share to twitter
  • share to linkedin
  • email article
  • by John Gever John Gever, Managing Editor, MedPage Today November 06, 2019

In another courtroom defeat for the Trump administration, a federal district judge determined that a rule issued by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) in May 2019, "Protecting Statutory Conscience Rights in Health Care," is unconstitutional.

Judge Paul A. Engelmayer of New York's Southern District issued a 147-page decision that "vacates HHS's 2019 Rule in its entirety."

The HHS rule would have allowed healthcare professionals to refuse to perform treatments or provide counseling or referrals "on account of religious beliefs or moral convictions." Although the main intent appeared to center on abortion, critics worried that it could also reduce patients' access to contraception, physician-assisted suicide, and even vaccinations, given that "moral convictions" were left up to the individual provider to define.

"The Conscience Provisions [in existing statutes] recognize and protect undeniably important rights," Engelmayer wrote. But the way the administration went about trying to enforce them violated the federal Administrative Procedures Act as well as the Constitution, he said.

At this writing, the administration has not said whether it will appeal the ruling.

The decision was in response to three separate lawsuits seeking to overturn the rule, which the court consolidated into a single case. One of the suits was filed by a coalition of blue-state attorneys general and local officials in Chicago; another by Planned Parenthood's national office and its northern New England chapter; and the third came from another pair of family planning organizations. The rule was set to go into effect later this month.

As one might expect, abortion rights groups applauded the ruling. "No one should be concerned about being denied the medical care they need simply because of their health care provider's religious, moral, or personal beliefs," said Planned Parenthood's acting president and CEO, Alexis McGill Johnson, in a statement released by the National Women's Law Center.

Details

Two sets of arguments underpinned the plaintiffs' case. One was procedural: that the "Conscience Provisions" to which Engelmayer referred -- stipulations in several existing laws that aimed to protect against religious discrimination on the job, which HHS claimed as its statutory authority -- don't give HHS the authority "to promulgate regulations with the force of law, or to withhold all federal funds for violating such laws," as the judge put it in summarizing the plaintiffs' arguments. Additionally, the administration's process for developing the rule failed to follow established procedures and violated a number of other statutes, including the Affordable Care Act.

The other set was constitutional. One of these was grounded in the First Amendment, in that the rule requires employers "to conform their business practices to the religious practices of their employees." Another argument was that it violates the Fifth Amendment in being too vague and in depriving patients of privacy and liberty rights. Still others held that the the rule's main stick -- withholding federal funds from violators -- breached the separation of powers.

But their main argument, as Engelmayer put it, was that the rule would "add, by regulatory fiat, major new substantive content" to the existing laws, "expand[ing] the meaning of core statutory terms" and granting HHS unprecedented enforcement powers. HHS countered that the rule was merely "housekeeping" meant to clarify and consolidate the various Conscience Provisions into a single, easily understood format as they apply to healthcare.

Engelmayer, an Obama appointee, sided with the plaintiffs on that one. "HHS's characterization of the Rule as solely ministerial cannot be taken seriously," he wrote. HHS's lawyer acknowledged at a hearing that the rule vastly broadens the range of activities that could be considered objectionable -- such as transporting a patient to an appointment where abortion may be merely discussed.

One real-life case from the 1990s mentioned in the litigation involved an emergency-department nurse who had twice refused to participate in procedures on pregnant women — one with placenta previa who was “standing in a pool of blood” — because the nurse believed the treatments could terminate the pregnancies. In the latter instance, her refusal led to a half-hour delay in treatment. Afterward, she refused reassignment to a neonatal ICU where such situations wouldn’t arise again, so the hospital fired her.

She then filed a lawsuit alleging her religious freedom had been infringed, but it was dismissed because the hospital had offered a “reasonable accommodation” as allowed under Title VII. HHS’s lawyer conceded to Engelmayer that, had the “conscience rule” been in place, the hospital could have been found in violation and lost all HHS funding.

Ultimately, HHS lacks the authority to make the changes outlined in the rule, or to enforce them by withholding all HHS funding, Engelmayer ruled. This was not only because of the rulemaking missteps, but also because HHS was essentially assuming powers never granted to it by Congress.

He also ridiculed HHS's rationale for issuing the rule in the first place -- an upswing in complaints from healthcare workers related to morally objectionable activities. In issuing the rule, HHS had claimed that it had received 358 complaints during the 2 years ending in September 2018. But under questioning, HHS's lawyer conceded that most involved issues untouched by the rule, and ultimately could point to just 11 to which the Conscience Provisions might apply.

"This conceded fact is fatal to HHS's stated justification for the Rule," Engelmayer wrote. He also noted that some of the complaints involved provision of contraception, which the Conscience Provisions -- again, the sole statutory basis for the "conscience rule" -- do not address. That HHS cited them in support of the rule serves as another example of the agency's overreach, the judge suggested.

Engelmayer was also harsh in criticizing HHS's blanket statement that the rule could live "harmoniously" with EMTALA, the federal law requiring emergency departments to treat all patients, without explaining how.

"The comments received by HHS in response to the draft Rule -- many in detail, many from medical personnel with duties to emergency patients -- should have yielded a thoughtful response from the federal agency responsible for health care, one that engaged with these important questions. HHS did not provide such a response. HHS's 'generalized conclusions' and inadequate responses to these professionals virtually define the [regulatory] term 'arbitrary and capricious,'" Engelmayer wrote.

He did back HHS in one respect, ruling that plaintiffs' arguments against the rule on First Amendment grounds were faulty. He noted that, because the rule treated "secular" moral objections as equal to religious ones, it cannot be considered to reflect the establishment of religion prohibited by the amendment.
