Bill Nye — It’s Worse Than You Imagined

Rev. Mr. Matthew Newsome
Apr 26, 2017 · 7 min read

Science. The word denotes logic. Reason. Cold, hard facts. Measurable data and observable phenomena. Objectivity. It’s good, then, that Bill Nye is no longer calling himself “The Science Guy” on his new Netflix series, which is lacking in all of the above.

I subjected myself to watching one episode of Bill Nye Saves the World. Rather than start at the beginning, I skipped to episode nine, “The Sexual Spectrum,” because I wanted to see how bad it could possibly be. It’s worse than I imagined.

If you think Nye’s new show would be anything like his popular 90’s children’s educational show, Bill Nye the Science Guy, you would be mistaken. Nye’s latest endeavor is a talk-show style format devoid of any sort of scientific experiments or demonstrations, presenting only his and his guests’ opinions on various subject matter.

The only “science” mentioned in the episode about sex comes during the first segment when we are informed about sex chromosomes and their function. If you are XX you are female, and if you are XY you are male. Most all of us learned this in school. But Nye is quick to point out that this binary way of looking at things is outdated. Some people have more than two sex chromosomes. Some have only one. Indeed, he instructs us, people don’t really fall into binary categories of male or female, but exist somewhere on a sexual spectrum with male at one end and female at the other.

Nye mentions that there are abnormalities in the sex chromosomes in 1 in 400 pregnancies, which he calls “quite a lot.” Calling it a “spectrum” suggests that most of us would be neither XX or XY but something in between. Generally the extreme ends of the spectrum represent the minority with most people somewhere in the middle.

So what is the rate of these genetic variations (what we used to call “mutations”)? Bill Nye doesn’t say. From what I could find quickly online:

  • X0 (only a single X chromosome): 1 in 2,000 to 1 in 5,000 females.
  • XXX: 1 in 1,000 females.
  • XXY: 1 in 500 to 1 in 1,000 males.
  • XYY: 1 in 900 to 1 in 2,000 males.

In other words, even the most common sexual chromosomal genetic mutation occurs in less than one-tenth of a percent of the population. The overwhelming majority of human beings are either XX or XY. It’s important to note, as well, that in all of the above cases the person will still be male (XXY or XYY) or female (X0 or XXX). People who are truly intersex — neither male nor female but of an indeterminate sex — are even more rare. You can find some of the numbers on the page of the Intersex Society of North America (link provided for reference).

None of this information is new to science. We’ve known about the existence of sex chromosomes for some time now, and we’ve known that sometimes abnormalities occur — just like with any of our genetic information. What is new is the suggestion that because variations occur in a tiny percentage of the population, then the whole idea of “normal” should be thrown out the window.

It is telling that we don’t seem to do this with anything that doesn’t have to do with sex. A small minority of people are born with only one arm, or a partial arm. But we all understand that it is normal for humans to have two arms. The fact that abnormalities occur does not negate the norm or render the idea of the norm meaningless. What is more telling, that 399 out of 400 children are born with typical XX or XY chromosomes or that 1 out of 400 is not?

Enough of that. Bill Nye does not go all that deep into the genetics of sex or its implication for society. Nor does he address the problems that are often incumbent with such genetic abnormalities, such as sterility. He simply says it’s a “spectrum” and we should all be happy about that.

But wait, there’s more! Biological sex is only one aspect of the spectrum. There is also gender, which Nye defines as “how you identify yourself and your experience,” which may be male, female, both or neither. Then there is attraction, which has to do with who you are attracted to (male, female, both, or neither). Finally there is expression, which is how you present yourself to the world (as a male, female, both or neither). Confused yet?

Bill Nye did not invent these categories. Anyone who has been exposed to modern gender theory will have heard of them before. What you might expect from “the Science Guy” is some discussion about whether these concepts are rooted in hard science. Nye does not offer any evidence that they are.

So we are left with the odd conclusion that it may be possible to be a biological male, who identifies as a male, is attracted to females, but who chooses to express himself to the world as a female. Or a biological female who expresses herself as a female, is attracted to males, but identifies herself as a male. Indeed, with all these variations, the implication is that most people are a complex blend of sexuality with very few lining up perfectly all along one side or the other. But what do we observe in the world around us? Mostly people whose sex, gender, attraction and expression correspond as expected. Somehow that is not scientifically relevant.

Having defined the terms, in the second segment of the show Nye delves into the bizarre world of Korean pop music (where we are told, among other things, that “conservative” people are always going to object to “progress”). We are then offered a segment called “Bill Nye Needs a Minute” where he just sort of rants on stage and yells at people who don’t agree with his presentation of sexuality to “Just get over it, will you?” to a round of applause.

Next he interviews three so-called “experts” that include a professor of “Gender and Sexuality Studies,” a cultural anthropologist, and (wait for it)… a comedian. I have to note that two of the three panelists appear male and one appears female, though based on the information offered during the first segment, you wouldn’t assume I’d be able to tell that.

The most telling moment of the panel discussion is when the anthropologist points out that there is no “gay gene” and that the idea of “homosexuality” is only about 100 years old. While homosexual acts have been around much, much longer, it is only very recently in our culture that we have developed the idea of a homosexual identity. To me this suggests that our concept of homosexuality is more of a cultural construct than anything biological, but the panelists don’t draw this conclusion.

The saddest moment in the discussion is when the professor of Gender and Sexuality Studies claims to not know whether or not his three-year-old son is a boy. I suppose it takes a PhD to unlearn what any farmer grows up knowing.

The last half of the program is just drivel. It features a cartoon making fun of people who hold to traditional sexual ethics — especially religious people — by depicting them as “vanilla” ice cream. And finally we are treated to a poorly produced song and dance by Rachel Bloom of Crazy Ez-Girlfriend fame. In case you are not familiar with her show, it features — like so many television shows today — sexually immature 20-somethings and their inability to form meaningful adult relationships. The show has its funny moments, but ends up just being depressing as the characters refuse to learn any lessons from their bad behavior.

Bloom’s song about “versatile love” on Bill Nye’s show is not funny — not even in a 13-year-old adolescent body humor way. It’s certainly not educational. I wouldn’t even call it “sexy” or provocative. It’s just obscene.

Bill Nye used to have a pretty good children’s educational TV show. For those readers who grew up watching him in the 90s and may be thinking of introducing your children to his new Netflix show, please DON’T. Bill Nye is not suitable for children. Netflix gives this new series a TV-14 rating, but this episode at least deserves a TV-MA rating. I wouldn’t subject my children to it (showing them Bloom’s song at the end alone would be tantamount to child abuse) and frankly I’m sorry I subjected myself to it.

There is some value in understanding how the modern secular world thinks about sexuality. I was hoping that Bill Nye’s show might at least offer that. But it offered nothing. There are means of obtaining that information that are far less offensive, better researched, better presented, and frankly more entertaining.

Surprisingly, for someone who supposedly champions reason, not once in this episode did Nye even raise the question What is sex for? Why are human beings sexual? There is no mention of children; no mention of marriage; no mention of love. No mention, in fact, of any sort of consequences for our sexual behavior. Bill Nye speaks in this episode of how things are “in the real world” but one gets the impression that he hasn’t lived there in quite some time.

The show ends in what I can only describe as a moment of paralyzing irony. Bill Nye offers a closing monologue during which he says:

“Science is the process by which we understand nature, by which we understand our place in the world, how we all fit in.”

The total lack of anything resembling an objective, scientific analysis of the nature of human sexuality in the preceding 25 minutes doesn’t seem to bother Bill Nye one bit.
