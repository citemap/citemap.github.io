Milestones: US Federal Agency activity on LGBT Health and Welfare 2009 to 2016

The current Federal recognition and treatment of gender non-conforming youth in the US has been enabled by Federal research and policy initiatives in the domains of public health, education, and civil rights.

2009

In 2009 the NIH commissioned the IOM for a LGBT research survey and directional proposal

2010

2010  Pres. Obama issues a letter to HHS requesting a plan to improve LGBT health and well being

2011

2011  Secretary Kathleen Sebelius responds with a proposed directive & charters, while the OCR recognizes Transgender students in comments on an earlier 2011 DCL

2012

Those new offices were established in 2012 , along with the introduction of Gender Identity, replacing sex, as a basis of non-discrimination policy.

2013

Which informed the 2013 NIH RFI towards strategic goals and the the OCRs work on ACA integration

2014

Which were refined and extended by HHS offices, and service providers in 2014

2015

Reinforced in 2015, in preparation for codification, and released as a rules proposal by the HHS

2016

And legally recognized in 2016 as possessing substantive rights, backed up by agency priority and  planning

 

Notes:

The current US Federal definition, identification, legal status, and civil rights recognition of gender non-conforming children and adolescents is largely the result of Federal HHS initiatives commmenced during the Obama administration. Those began in 2009 within the NIH, became an agency-wide directive in 2012, and gained formal civil rights protections in 2016 with rule making by the OCR.

While the broader set of initiatives and policies from that period were well warranted and have been beneficial to both public and personal welfare, and the competency of healthcare providers, they have also produced unintended consequences. Because they have made the gender identity of children and adolescents the significant dimension of their medical identification and its interpretation under civil rights and disability law .
