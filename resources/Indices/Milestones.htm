Milestones: US Federal Agency activity on LGBT Health and Welfare 2009 to 2016

The legal and medical status of gender non-conforming youth in the US has largely been the result of Federal research and policy initiatives in the domains of public health, education, and civil rights occurring from approximately 2009 to the present.

The table below highlights key milestones in the evolution of contemporary law and policy as it affects gender non-conforming minors and their families, along with the educational and clinical professionals they may interact with.

2009

The NIH commissions the IOM for a  thorough LGBT health research survey and gap analysis

2010

Enabling Pres. Obama to issue a request to HHS for plans to improve LGBT health and wellbeing.  

To be facilitated by enhanced Title IX,, ADA, and Sect.504 rights and  protections for LGBT citizens.

                                                                                                                                                    * now recognizing gender identity’ as a novel factor.

2011

Secretary Kathleen Sebelius proposes a slate of directives and new charters. Soon after, the OCR adduces gender-based factors germane to sexual harassment and sexual violence. reviving a 2001 line of analysis.

2012

Those new offices were established in 2012 and able to scale their activities.

2013

Issuing a Specific Health Issues RFI to prepare a foundation for the 2016-20  program strategy, lead by the  to be complemented by  the OCR’s work on ACA integration

2014

Which were refined and extended by HHS offices, and service providers

2015

Reinforced in 2015, in preparation for codification, and released as a rules proposal by the HHS

2016

And legally recognized in 2016 as possessing substantive rights, backed up by agency priority and  planning

 

Notes:

The current US Federal definition, identification, legal status, and civil rights recognition of gender non-conforming children and adolescents is largely the result of Federal HHS initiatives commmenced during the Obama administration. Those began in 2009 within the NIH, became an agency-wide directive in 2012, and gained formal civil rights protections in 2016 with rule making by the OCR.

While the broader set of initiatives and policies from that period were well warranted and have been beneficial to both public and personal welfare, and the competency of healthcare providers, they have also produced unintended consequences. Because they have made the gender identity of children and adolescents the significant dimension of their medical identification and its interpretation under civil rights and disability law .
